LIRR AND MNR REPORT


During the work week, the number of people in Manhattan swells to about double the number of people who live there, and the majority (about 81%) of people who travel into Manhattan take mass transit. The LIRR and MNR train networks are not only beneficial, they are absolutely essential to transportation into and out of Manhattan. This report analyzes challenges facing the Long Island Rail Road and Metro-North Railroad, and makes the case that improving these vital transit lines is the most viable, if not the only, way to improve transportation for commuters living along the corridors that these train lines serve.

To understand how crucial the LIRR and MNR are, it is necessary to view them in the context of the broader transportation network. The LIRR and MNR systems cover an extensive area with over 700 route miles combined, dozens of branches, and 248 stations. Combined, the MNR and LIRR brought 229,162 commuters into Grand Central Terminal and Penn Station on an average weekday in 2017. This is about 14 percent of the total number of people commuting into Manhattan. The prohibitive costs of car ownership, insurance, gas, and storing a vehicle in Manhattan, coupled with heavy traffic and the limited capacity of Manhattan streets and parking infrastructure, make driving to work infeasible for the majority of LIRR and MNR commuters.

The majority of street space in Manhattan is dedicated to moving and storing private vehicles, only a minority of people commuting into Manhattan do so in cars, and despite these facts, vehicle congestion has worsened in recent years, with Midtown core taxi speeds averaging a dismal 5 mph, (NYC Mobility Report, pg 18). There is not enough space in Manhattan’s business district for the number of drivers entering today, and the number of people commuting into Manhattan is projected to grow. The LIRR and MNR are the most feasible way to carry such a large number of people into the city from the suburbs. Perhaps this is most evident in the fact that despite an increase in delays and train cancellations, LIRR and MNR ridership has grown to its highest level in decades. Addressing the challenges these systems face and ensuring that they have the funding they need to maintain their equipment, replace old, unreliable equipment, and complete their planned capital projects on schedule, benefits the city’s economic health and the quality of life of commuters from the suburbs and local residents alike.
